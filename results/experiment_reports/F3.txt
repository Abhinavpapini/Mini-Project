=====================================================================
WHY GRL WORKS — ANALYSIS
=====================================================================

1. SPEAKER BIAS IN HuBERT FEATURES:
   WomenWhoStutter (9163 clips, 43.8%) dominates the dataset.
   Without disentanglement, the CNN learns show-specific acoustic
   patterns (recording quality, microphone, speaker voice) that
   correlate with stutter types only coincidentally.
   GRL forces the encoder to ignore show identity → features become
   more generalisable to unseen speaking conditions.

2. BALANCED IMPROVEMENT ACROSS ALL CLASSES:
   Unlike previous methods where some types gain and others lose,
   F3 improves ALL 5 stutter types simultaneously:
   All 5 classes achieve their best F1 of the entire B/F-series,
   and WordRep/SoundRep achieve new highs across the project.

3. SPEAKER ADVERSARY CONVERGES TO NEAR-CHANCE (loss=1.37):
   Chance CE loss for 5 equal classes = -log(1/5) = 1.609
   Speaker loss starts at 1.608 (≈ chance), drops slightly until
   GRL kicks in, then stabilises at ~1.37 (near-chance).
   This means the encoder successfully obfuscates show information.
   Speaker loss NOT going to 1.609 exactly = some residual show info;
   still enough disentanglement to substantially improve classification.

4. GRL λ WARM-UP IS CRITICAL:
   Without warmup (λ=0.5 from ep1), the adversary would immediately
   destroy the stutter signal before the encoder learns useful features.
   The linear ramp (0→0.5 over 10 epochs) allows stutter classification
   to initially converge (ep1-5 show rapid F1 improvement from 0.16→0.39)
   before the adversarial pressure is fully applied (ep10+ → 0.49+).

5. CONSISTENT IMPROVEMENT ACROSS 50 EPOCHS (unlike other methods):
   Previous methods (CNN baseline, B-series) peaked at ep7-20 then
   oscillated. F3 shows monotonic improvement from ep1 to ep47,
   reaching 0.540. Full convergence likely requires 70-100 epochs.

Key Hypotheses and Expected Outcomes
------------------------------------
- H1: Layer optimality is stutter-type dependent: Blocks (motor planning failure)
   may be better captured in later layers encoding prosodic structure, while sound
   repetitions (acoustic-phonetic events) may peak in mid-layers. Expected: per-type
   optimal layers differ by 3 to 8 layers.
- H2: Dimensionality reduction improves generalization: PCA to about 32 dimensions
   from 1024 should improve macro-F1 on minority stutter types by removing noise
   dimensions, similar to the 32-dim result in the dysarthria paper.
- H3: Handcrafted stutter features provide complementary signal: Features like
   jitter, pause ratio, and speaking rate should show low correlation with compact
   SSL+MFCC fusion (similar to <0.1 mean correlation in the dysarthria paper),
   indicating additive value.
- H4: Graph-based models outperform CNN on temporal stutter patterns: Disfluencies
   span multiple frames and involve non-local acoustic dependencies (for example,
   coarticulation changes before a block). GCN/D-GAT are expected to capture these
   better than frame-averaged CNN classifiers.
- H5: Multi-label formulation improves co-occurrence handling: Converting to
   multi-label binary classification should improve F1 on clips with two or more
   simultaneous disfluency annotations, expected to be a significant portion of
   SEP-28k.
